## 1. Abstract (target ~180 words; two versions, pick after experiments resolve)

**Conservative form (defensible from data in hand today).**
> Three-class sentiment classification (negative / neutral / positive) is routinely bottlenecked
> by neutral scarcity: real-world corpora — product reviews, social media, survey verbatims —
> contain few cleanly-neutral instances, and most public benchmarks are binary. Using sentence
> embeddings fed to a gradient-boosted classifier (XGBoost), we vary the proportion of neutral
> training instances from 1% to 99% while holding training-set size fixed, with ten seeded
> replications per condition and a fixed held-out validation set. We find a non-monotonic
> ("inverted-U") relationship: positive- and negative-class F1 are *not* maximised when neutral
> data is minimised. Both peak at an intermediate neutral share (≈25–40%) and degrade at both
> extremes, while neutral-class F1 rises and then plateaus. The result reframes neutral data as
> carrying information that sharpens — not merely competes with — the positive/negative decision
> boundary, and quantifies the proportion that jointly optimises all three classes.

**Strong form (only if the §6 synthetic-vs-real arm replicates the effect).**
> …We further show that **synthetic** neutral instances generated by a large language model,
> controlled for topic, register, and readability, reproduce the cross-class benefit of real
> neutral data, offering a practical remedy for neutral scarcity that requires no additional
> human annotation. The effect holds across N embedding backends and is robust to
> contamination and overlap checks.

**Do not ship the strong form** until the contamination checks (§7) and the synthetic-vs-real
baseline (§6, arm D) are complete. The conservative form is already supported.

## 2. Introduction — the neutral-scarcity framing

Three points, in order, ground the paper without over-claiming.

**2.1 Neutral is the structurally hard, structurally scarce class.** Most large public
sentiment corpora are *binary* by construction: IMDb and Amazon polarity derive labels from
star ratings collapsed to high/low; Sentiment140 distant-supervises labels from emoticons.
Neutral is either absent or defined residually. Where a neutral class exists (e.g. the
three-way SemEval Twitter tasks, Financial PhraseBank), it is frequently the minority and the
hardest to annotate reliably, because "neutral" conflates *objective/factual*, *mixed*, and
*affect-absent* texts that are not one phenomenon. This is the gap the paper sits in. (Cite,
with real verification before submission: Rosenthal et al., SemEval-2017 Task 4; Malo et al.,
2014, Financial PhraseBank; Go, Bhayani & Huang, 2009, Sentiment140; Maas et al., 2011, IMDb.)

**2.2 The intuition the data overturns.** A practitioner reasoning informally expects neutral
data to *only* trade off against the other classes — more neutral examples should help neutral
recall and hurt positive/negative by "stealing" decision space. The existing experiment shows
this intuition is wrong at the relevant operating point: starving the model of neutral data
*also* degrades positive- and negative-class F1. Neutral instances appear to act partly as
**hard negatives** that define where the positive and negative regions *end*. Without them the
boundary is placed by extrapolation and the polar classes bleed into the neutral region they
never saw.

**2.3 Why "synthetic neutral" is the contribution, and why it must be argued narrowly.**
LLM-based data augmentation for text classification is a crowded area (2023–2025). The
defensible novelty is **not** "LLMs can augment data" (well known) but the **specific,
measured, cross-class effect of *neutral* augmentation on a three-class sentiment boundary**,
the **non-monotonic dose-response curve** that locates the optimum, and **whether controlled
synthetic neutrals recover the same effect as real ones**. Framed this narrowly it is novel and
useful; framed broadly a reviewer buries it under prior augmentation work (Wei & Zou, 2019,
EDA; Sennrich et al., 2016, back-translation; the GPT-augmentation literature). Keep the claim
scoped to the neutral class and the three-way boundary geometry.

**Positioning relative to known results to cite honestly (verify each before submission):**
class-imbalance learning (He & Garcia, 2009); SMOTE (Chawla et al., 2002) and its text-domain
limitations (interpolating in embedding space can create off-manifold neutrals); the general
finding that minority-class augmentation can help majority classes via better margin
estimation; and the "neutral is heterogeneous" point from sentiment-annotation literature.

## 3. What is already established (and exactly what is not)

This section is the integrity core. State it plainly in the paper's Methods/Limitations.

### 3.1 The result we have

Source: `test_methods/test_neutral_percentage.R` + `multiclass_neutral_percentage_results.csv`
(USE embeddings; sub-sampled ≤1000 obs per sentiment×source cell; 80/20 split; **10 seeded runs
per condition** with `set.seed(i*100+run)`; **fixed** validation set; XGBoost `multi:softprob`,
depth 6, eta 0.3, 50 rounds; F1 per class averaged over runs). Measured mean per-class F1 as the
**proportion of neutral data in the training set** is swept (total size held constant):

| % neutral in train | F1 positive | F1 negative | F1 neutral |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 1   | 0.586 | 0.785 | — (n/a) |
| 8.3 | 0.657 | 0.795 | 0.424 |
| 16.7| 0.720 | 0.813 | 0.676 |
| 25  | 0.758 | 0.827 | 0.770 |
| **33.3**| **0.766** | 0.826 | 0.806 |
| 41.7| 0.764 | 0.823 | 0.820 |
| 50  | 0.738 | 0.814 | 0.816 |
| 58.3| 0.696 | 0.798 | 0.800 |
| 66.7| 0.618 | 0.764 | 0.769 |
| 75  | 0.550 | 0.729 | 0.743 |
| 83.3| 0.430 | 0.668 | 0.706 |
| 91.7| 0.265 | 0.503 | 0.652 |
| 99  | 0.014 | 0.040 | 0.575 |

**The defensible findings, stated as-is:**
1. Positive-class F1 is **non-monotonic** in neutral share — it climbs from 0.586 (1% neutral)
   to a peak ≈0.766 at ~33%, then falls. **Minimising neutral data does not maximise
   positive-class F1.** This is the headline and it is real in this experiment.
2. Negative-class F1 shows the same shape, peaking ≈0.827 at ~25–33%, lower at both 1% and at
   high neutral shares.
3. Neutral-class F1 rises monotonically then plateaus/declines slightly past ~42%.
4. A single intermediate neutral share (≈33–42%) **jointly** sits near the maximum for all three
   classes — i.e. there is a co-optimum, not a pure trade-off, over the useful range.
5. A **classification-vs-regression** comparison exists (`regression_neutral_percentage_results.csv`,
   ordinal target on the same sweep): regression flattens the positive/negative inverted-U (peaks
   lower, ≈0.667/0.754, but degrades far more gracefully at high neutral shares). This is a real,
   reportable secondary finding about *how the loss interacts with neutral proportion*, not noise.

### 3.2 What this experiment does NOT show (the gap to close)

> **Critical honesty flag.** The curve above varies the proportion of **real** neutral instances
> (USE-embedded, drawn from the real corpora). It is a *dose-response for neutral proportion*, not
> a test of **synthetic** augmentation. The production models *do* train on GPT-4o synthetic
> neutrals (23,962 generated, controlled for topic/style/figurative-language/Flesch reading-ease;
> see `synthetic data/create_synthetic_data_with_gpt4o.py`), and the v2 roadmap attributes the
> neutral-class lift to them — **but no run yet isolates synthetic-neutral against real-neutral at
> matched proportion.** Therefore the strong-form abstract claim ("*synthetic* neutral data raises
> pos/neg F1") is **currently an inference, not a measured contrast.**

Also not yet shown, and required:
- **No confidence intervals reported** even though 10 runs exist — only run-means are saved. Re-run
  to emit per-condition SD/CI and significance of the inverted-U vs. the 1%-neutral baseline.
- **Single embedding backend** (USE only) for the dose-response; generalisation across e5/bge/gte/
  OpenAI is asserted in the roadmap but not measured *for the neutral effect* (the bake-off varied
  the embedder at a fixed mix; this experiment varies the mix at a fixed embedder — neither crosses
  them).
- **No baseline contrast** to class weights, undersampling, or SMOTE — so we cannot yet say
  synthetic augmentation *beats* the cheap rebalancing fixes, only that neutral proportion matters.
- **No mechanism evidence** (is the gain a sharper margin? a hard-negative effect? an embedding-
  space coverage effect?).
- **No contamination audit** (test-set overlap of synthetic text; LLM pretraining leakage of public
  test sets).
- **Sub-sampling artefact risk:** capping ≤1000/cell and holding *total* size constant means high
  neutral shares *starve* the polar classes in absolute count; part of the right-hand collapse is
  trivially "too few positives/negatives," not a neutral effect. The hardened design must separate
  *neutral proportion* from *polar absolute count* (see §6, design note).

## 4. Research questions (what the hardened paper answers)

- **RQ1 (dose-response).** How does per-class F1 depend on neutral training proportion, with CIs,
  and where is the joint optimum? *(Have it; needs CIs + the absolute-count confound removed.)*
- **RQ2 (synthetic equivalence).** Do controlled LLM-synthetic neutrals reproduce the cross-class
  benefit of *real* neutrals at matched proportion? *(Core new contribution; not yet run.)*
- **RQ3 (vs. cheaper remedies).** Does adding (real or synthetic) neutral data beat class weights,
  undersampling, and SMOTE on neutral-scarce corpora? *(Not yet run.)*
- **RQ4 (generalisation).** Does the effect hold across embedding backends (e5-small/base, bge,
  gte, OpenAI-3-small, legacy USE) and across neutral-heavy corpora (esp. Financial PhraseBank)?
- **RQ5 (mechanism).** Is the cross-class gain attributable to hard-negative margin sharpening or
  to embedding-space coverage? *(Exploratory.)*

## 5. Data

- **Real mixed corpora** (already assembled): Sentiment140, IMDb, Amazon reviews, financial news,
  TweetSemEval, COVID surveys, reviewshake. Used for training and same-domain test.
- **Neutral-heavy corpora for the 3-class story** (the audience-critical ones):
  - **Financial PhraseBank** (Malo et al., 2014) — 3-class, neutral-dominant, four annotator-
    agreement subsets; report on the ≥75% and 100%-agreement subsets. *This is the corpus that
    makes the neutral contribution legible to reviewers; binary benchmarks hide it.*
  - SemEval-2017 Task 4-A (3-class Twitter) for cross-domain.
  - COVID-survey verbatims (in-house) as a held-out, **non-public** set the embedders cannot have
    memorised — doubles as the contamination control (§7).
- **Synthetic neutrals**: 23,962 GPT-4o generations with logged controls (topic ∈ ~150 incl.
  workplace/survey/product/policy themes; register {formal, informal}; figurative {present, none};
  target Flesch Reading Ease level with *measured* FRES/FK-grade per item). Provenance, prompt, and
  per-item metadata are retained — a publication asset (reproducibility) **and** an audit surface.

**Label hygiene.** Define "neutral" operationally and report the three neutral sub-types where
recoverable (factual/objective vs. mixed vs. affect-absent); annotation heterogeneity of neutral
is a known confound and a reviewer will ask.

## 6. Hardened experimental design (the matrix that makes it publishable)

Fix everything except the manipulated factor. **Replications: ≥10 seeds** (already the cadence;
extend to ≥20 for the headline contrasts). Report **mean ± 95% bootstrap CI** per class and
**macro-F1**, with paired tests (bootstrap or McNemar on the fixed test set) for every contrast.

**Design note — break the proportion/count confound.** Run the dose-response in *two* modes:
(i) **total-fixed** (current: polar count shrinks as neutral grows — keep for comparability) and
(ii) **polar-fixed** (hold positive and negative absolute counts constant; *add* neutral on top).
Mode (ii) isolates "what does adding neutral do" from "what does removing polar data do," and is
the honest test of the cross-class claim. Report both; the §3.1 right-hand collapse is partly a
mode-(i) artefact and saying so pre-empts the reviewer.

**Arms (each at the matched neutral proportions, both modes, all backends in §6.4):**
- **A. Baseline — no neutral / minimal neutral.** Floor.
- **B. Real neutral.** The §3.1 condition, now with CIs and mode (ii).
- **C. Class weights / focal loss** at neutral floor — the "fix imbalance without new data" arm.
- **D. SMOTE / borderline-SMOTE** on neutral in embedding space — the "synthesise without an LLM"
  arm. (Expectation, to be tested: off-manifold neutrals; weaker than real.)
- **E. Undersampling** polar classes to the neutral count — the trivial rebalancer.
- **F. Synthetic neutral (LLM).** The contribution. **F vs B** = synthetic-equals-real test (RQ2);
  **F vs C/D/E** = beats-cheap-remedies test (RQ3).
- **G. Real + synthetic neutral.** Does augmentation stack with available real neutral?

**6.4 Embedding backends (RQ4).** Cross every arm with: e5-small (v2 default), e5-base (best
on-device), OpenAI-3-small (API), and legacy USE (for continuity with §3.1). bge-base/gte-base as
robustness. Note e5 requires the `query:`/`passage:` prefix — apply consistently or it confounds.

**6.5 Classifier.** XGBoost `multi:softprob` as primary (matches the package). Hold hyper-params
fixed across arms; report one sensitivity sweep. Single-threaded dev-Mac XGBoost (no OpenMP) is a
*speed* caveat only, not a correctness one — state it so timing numbers aren't misread.

**6.6 Mechanism probes (RQ5, exploratory, clearly labelled).**
- 2-D projection (UMAP/PCA) of the embedding space with/without neutral training data; show whether
  added neutrals tighten the polar margins (hard-negative reading) vs. merely fill the centre.
- Decision-boundary error analysis: of polar test items misclassified at 1% neutral, what fraction
  are corrected at the optimum, and do they sit near the neutral region? A high fraction supports
  the hard-negative mechanism.
- Report this as *evidence consistent with*, not proof of, the mechanism.

## 7. Contamination and leakage checks (a reviewer **will** demand these)

Two distinct threats; address both explicitly.

1. **Synthetic ↔ test overlap.** The synthetic neutrals must not leak into or duplicate test items.
   Checks: exact + near-duplicate detection (MinHash/embedding cosine ≥ threshold) between every
   synthetic item and every test item; report the overlap rate and *remove* matches before training.
   Because synthetic generation logs topic/seed, also confirm no test corpus was used as a prompt.
2. **LLM-pretraining leakage of public test sets.** OpenAI and sentence-transformer embedders may
   have seen Sentiment140 / IMDb / SemEval during pretraining, inflating same-domain scores. Cannot
   be eliminated, so *bound* it: include the **non-public COVID-survey** held-out set (and, if
   feasible, a freshly-scraped recent set post-dating model cut-offs) and show the neutral effect
   **persists on data the models cannot have memorised**. Report public-vs-private deltas honestly.
3. **Synthetic realism / distribution check.** Show synthetic neutrals occupy the same embedding
   region as real neutrals (not an LLM-stylistic island): nearest-real-neighbour distance
   distributions, and a classifier that tries to separate real-vs-synthetic neutral (high
   separability = the synthetic set is detectable = a confound to disclose).

## 8. Reporting standards

- Macro-F1 **and** per-class F1 — never accuracy alone (accuracy hides the neutral collapse).
- Mean ± 95% CI over ≥10 (headline: ≥20) seeds; paired significance test for each contrast vs B.
- The full dose-response curve, both modes, per backend — figure 1 is the inverted-U with CI bands.
- Cost/latency per 1k docs per backend (on-device vs API): an on-brand, audience-relevant axis.
- Full reproducibility pack: seeds, splits, synthetic prompts + metadata, code. (arXiv + repo.)

## 9. Threats to validity / what is NOT yet proven (state in the paper)

- **Synthetic ≈ real is an open question** — asserted by the roadmap, not yet measured (arm F vs B).
  Until run, the paper's *measured* claim is the real-neutral dose-response (§3.1), full stop.
- **One classifier family.** XGBoost only; a linear probe / small MLP robustness check would
  strengthen the "it's the data, not the model" reading.
- **Neutral is heterogeneous;** results may depend on which neutral sub-type dominates a corpus.
- **Mode-(i) right-hand collapse is partly an absolute-count artefact** (acknowledged; mode (ii)
  fixes it).
- **External validity to long organisational text** (survey verbatims, exit interviews) is the
  brand-relevant claim but needs the org corpora to land cleanly — the applied/ORM angle is a
  *later*, co-authored paper, not this one.
- **No causal mechanism proven** — §6.6 probes are suggestive only.

## 10. Contribution statement (what to claim, exactly)

1. A seeded, CI-backed **dose-response** showing positive/negative-class F1 in 3-class sentiment is
   **non-monotonic** in neutral training proportion, with a **joint co-optimum** at an intermediate
   neutral share — overturning the "minimise neutral to protect the polar classes" intuition.
2. A controlled test of whether **LLM-synthetic neutrals recover the real-neutral benefit**, against
   class-weight / SMOTE / undersampling baselines, on **neutral-heavy** corpora (Financial PhraseBank).
3. **Contamination-audited** evidence (synthetic↔test overlap removed; effect shown on a non-public
   held-out set), with an open, reproducible pipeline and the synthetic corpus + provenance released.

Claims (1) and the design for (2)–(3) are ready; the *measured* strong-form of (2) is the gating
experiment. Submit conservative-form to arXiv now is viable only once CIs + mode-(ii) + the
Financial PhraseBank replication are in; the WASSA/BRM version waits on arm F.
